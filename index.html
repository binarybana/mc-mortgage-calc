Introduction

Often, we are interested in predicting some quantity in the future, but due to our imperfect knowledge of all the interacting elements, we have to deal with uncertainty. For example, say we are planning a party and are inviting over some friends. We plan on serving a delicious spaghetti dinner, but we are immediately faced with a dilemma: how much spaghetti should we make for our friends?

One way to tackle the problem is to make a blind guess, but the thought of wasting spaghetti, or not having enough, really grates us the wrong way. So we decide to take a more principled approach by breaking the problem down further in the hopes of simplifying it. One way to do this is to split the problem into estimating how many people are going to show up, and how much spaghetti the average guest will eat. Then we can multiply these numbers together to get an estimate for how much spaghetti they'll eat. In this classic 'divide-and-conquer' approach to problem solving, the idea is that the estimation of these simpler quantities is easier and something we can do more accurately than estimating the final number itself. But we can do one step better...

Instead of choosing a single number for each of these two quantities, we can instead embrace our uncertainty of these guesses and guess a probability distribution to describe each. For example, we might guess an expected amount of spaghetti that our average friend eats, but also realize that a few of our friends eat a great deal more, and some eat a lot less. We can quantify this uncertainty by answering questions such as: "I'm 90% sure that each of my friends will eat less than X cups of spaghetti."

Once we've done this for the amount of spaghetti each person eats, and the amount of friends that we think could come over, we can then effectively multiply these probability distributions together to obtain an estimated distribution of spaghetti that might get eaten at our party. In general this is difficult or even impossible to do using analytical (pencil and paper) methods, but using a computer, we can approximate the answer by 'simulating' thousands of parties and recording how much spaghhetti is eaten in each.
